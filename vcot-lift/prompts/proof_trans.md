# Instruction

You are an expert in Verus and Z3 SMT solver. You will be provided with a proof generated by Z3 and **a Verus program with proof hints**. 

The current Verus program contains only proof hints and does not expose the detailed internal verification steps required to establish correctness. Your task is to analyze the corresponding Z3 proof and *transform* its reasoning into explicit components of the Verus proof, thereby making the underlying verification steps concrete and explicit.

Verus proof consists of three components:

1. Assertions: conditions (assert) that must hold at specific program points.

2. Loop Invariants: conditions that hold true before and after each iteration of a loop.

3. Lemma Functions: reusable, proven properties (often defined with proof fn) that aid verification. 

# Knowledge

Z3 proof rules can be categorized into three levels based on their importance to transforming Z3 proofs into Verus proofs.

**High-importance rules** capture the core logical structure of the proof and usually correspond directly to explicit proof steps in Verus:

- `lemma`, `th-lemma`, `mp`, `mp~`, `unit-resolution`, `quant-intro`, `quant-inst`, `skolemize`

**Medium-importance rules** support reasoning and proof construction:

- `asserted`, `hypothesis`, `and-elim`, `not-or-elim`, `trans`, `trans*`, `monotonicity`, `der`, `rewrite`, `rewrite*`, `def-axiom`, `apply-def`

**Low-importance rules** primarily consist of tautologies, syntactic sugar, or proof metadata, and are logically trivial:

- `true`, `=`, `~`, `iff-true`, `iff-false`, `goal`, `refl`, `symm`, `commutativity`, `pull-quant`, `push-quant`, `elim-unused`, `distributivity`, `nnf-pos`, `nnf-neg`, `iff-oeq`, `def-intro`

When performing the transformation, priority should be given to **high-importance rules**, as they usually encode essential reasoning steps. For example, `mp`/`mp~` often correspond to intermediate logical deductions and can be transformed into `assert` statements in Verus. Similarly, `quant-inst` and `quant-intro` usually correspond to the introduction or application of `forall`-quantified statements, while `skolemize` often corresponds to reasoning about `exists`-quantified statements.
